Wild Rides

ed. note: Nothing in this blog post should be taken as statement of fact unless I specifically write “at ${company}, ${thing} happened”. Everything else is my personal opinion based on aggregate experiences.

The facts

In September 2022, Nearside was acquired by Plastiq, a company in the SMB/B2B payments space. In November, they announced that “due to macroeconomic conditions”, they would be shutting down all Nearside products - the Business Bank Account as well as Line of Credit ‚Äì by EOY, with most access being shut off in early December. Naturally, this also means that the Nearside mobile apps will be spun down as well.

Things I believe are true

  • Startups fail for any number of reasons, only some of which are under our control 1
  • I worked really darn hard for the past 2.5 years
  • I, as an well-intentioned, competent individual, am only capable of N amount of impact at a job (both positive and negative)

And yet…

I know my individual contribution was important (or else they wouldn’t have paid me!), but I also know that shit happens beyond my purview. I’ve worked on 9 apps/SDKs in my professional career (including Nearside), and for the most part, all of those apps still exist2. It’s wild to me that all the work we did over the past 2 years and 4 months just ceases to exist.

All the stuff I cared so deeply about ‚Äì docs, code comments, CI, linting rules, Gradle plugins ‚Äì it just vanishes.

“At least you made peoples’ lives better for a while!” some of you might say. Actually, I don’t know that that’s true! Imagine your bank told you that your account would close in 22 calendar days ‚Äì how chaotic would that be? Taking aside things like direct deposit and payroll, which take a minimum of 10 business days to change, there’s credit cards, utilities, quarterly recurring payments…the list goes on. As bad as I feel for all the work I did just going *poof*, I feel 100x worse that we made tens of thousands of small business owners’ lives measurably more complicated.

Them’s the breaks, right?

Yeah, for sure, I was under no such delusion that

  1. working at a startup (for the 4th time) would guarantee me stable employment3, or
  2. that this specific startup would ever amount to anything

Between you and me, I was sorta ü§ûüèΩhopingü§ûüèΩ that both items would be true. Still, there’s a difference between intellectually “knowing” that things could theoretically go south and having to actually run a layoff.

I’ve liked working in small groups for as long as I can remember. Group assignments in high school with 7 of us were intolerable, but I still fondly remember a few projects I got to work on in groups of 2 or 3. I suppose this (along with other reasons that I should broach in therapy) is why I really really enjoy working at startups. We’re all cogs in the capitalist machine; I just really enjoy being able to see the top of the machine from where I’m sitting!

And working as a cog at a startup can be such a joy! You want a coffee chat with the founder or COO? Yeah no problem, you just walk over to their desk (or ping them on Slack) and ask! It’s not weird, you already know each other ‚Äì in fact, they were probably on your interview loop!4.
You have inside jokes with senior leadership ‚Äì not because you’re special or cool or particularly funny, but because the company All Hands last week was 25 people and everyone is still allowed to be funny and not-serious without it setting the wrong tone. Finally, even well-funded startups are understaffed relative to their mission, and so the easiest way to curry favor with literally anyone is to throw a 15 min 1:1/sync on their calendar (with sufficient warning!) and ask “So how can I make your life easier?”.

Working at a small venture-funded company isn’t all unicorn farts and rainbow lollipops. VC funding itself is a tricky beast ‚Äì on one hand, it pays for our salaries that any reasonable person would call exorbitant – good luck paying rent in any major metro area otherwise! On the other hand, VC money is somewhat akin to the cursed Treasure of Cort√©s in Pirates of the Carribean ‚Äì you are damned by the action of taking it, not by the amount. Now, admittedly, that’s a bit melodramatic5, but taking VC money does mean you’re always looking and hoping for that next raise; your OKRs aren’t necessarily tied to profit, they might be tied to engagement or other traction-related metrics. When you do raise that next round, it’s often based on PMF rather than whether your business is actually sustainable. This, in my opinion, is the core of the VC-funding reality-distortion field that exists in tech.
